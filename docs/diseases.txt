# Oil Palm Diseases - Comprehensive Knowledge Base

## 1. Ganoderma Basal Stem Rot (GBSR)

### Overview
The most destructive disease of oil palm, caused by fungus *Ganoderma boninense*. Affects palms aged 4+ years.
Economic loss: Up to 40% of plantation yield.

### Symptoms
- White or orange mycelium at base of trunk (most visible)
- Constriction/girdling of trunk base
- Upper frond yellowing and wilting
- Reduced bunch production and quality
- Sudden collapse in advanced stage
- Root system shows white mycelium under bark
- Disease progression: 18-36 months from infection to death

### Cause & Transmission
- Fungus *Ganoderma boninense* (pathogenic species)
- Spores dispersed through soil contact, infected roots
- Spreads through root-to-root contact (avoid monoculture)
- Infection increases with waterlogging and poor drainage
- Prevalence: High in regions with >2500mm annual rainfall

### Treatment
- **Diseased Palm Removal**: Dig out infected palm with root ball to 1m depth
- **Burn/Compost**: Diseased material should NOT be left in field
- **Replanting Gap**: Leave 6-12 months before replanting
- **Fungicide (preventive only)**: Fludioxonil (not curative)
- **Biological Control**: Trichoderma spp. in planting holes
- **No Chemical Cure**: Once infected, palm must be removed

### Prevention (CRITICAL)
1. Proper drainage system (tile drains at 15-20m spacing)
2. Avoid waterlogging (main risk factor)
3. Mulching with organic matter (suppresses spores)
4. Crop rotation with non-susceptible crops
5. Quarantine procedures during planting
6. Select resistant clones (Tenera > Dura)
7. Deep plow to disrupt infected root fragments
8. Monitor young palms for early symptoms

### Yield Impact
- Infected palm: 0% production (death within 24-36 months)
- Prevention cost: $20-40/hectare/year
- Treatment cost: $50-100 per infected palm
- Replanting cost: $300-500 per palm

## 2. Leaf Spot Disease (Pestalotia/Cercospora)

### Overview
Common fungal disease affecting fronds. Caused by *Pestalotia palmarum* or *Cercospora elaeidis*.
Severity: Moderate (rarely fatal, but reduces photosynthesis)

### Symptoms
- Small, circular brown spots on fronds
- Spots may have concentric rings (target-like appearance)
- Necrotic centers with yellow halo
- Spots typically appear on older leaves first
- Premature frond senescence
- Reduced canopy coverage
- Progressive defoliation in severe cases

### Cause & Transmission
- Fungal spores dispersed by:
  * Rain splash
  * Wind (short distances)
  * Contact with contaminated tools
- High humidity and warm temperatures accelerate
- Poor canopy ventilation increases risk
- Stress from drought or nutrient deficiency increases susceptibility

### Treatment
- **Cultural Practices**: Remove infected fronds (lower leaves)
- **Fungicide Spray**: 
  * Mancozeb (0.2%) - broad spectrum
  * Chlorothalonil (0.15%) - effective preventive
  * Copper compounds (0.1%)
- **Application Timing**: Every 2-3 weeks during wet season
- **Canopy Thinning**: Improve air circulation through pruning

### Prevention
1. Maintain good drainage and air movement
2. Sanitize tools between trees
3. Remove affected fronds promptly
4. Apply fungicides prophylactically in wet season
5. Maintain plant vigor through irrigation and nutrition
6. Space palms adequately (9m x 9m minimum)
7. Use resistant varieties if available

### Yield Impact
- Mild infections: 5-10% yield reduction
- Severe infections: 15-25% yield reduction
- With treatment: Minimal long-term impact

## 3. Bunch Rot (Fusarium/Rhizopus)

### Overview
Rot of ripening fruit bunches. Caused by *Fusarium solani* or *Rhizopus spp.*
Critical during harvest season. Can cause 30% pre-harvest loss.

### Symptoms
- Rotting starting at fruit base or crown
- Black or dark brown discoloration
- Mushy, soft texture in affected fruits
- White or pink mycelium may be visible
- Foul smell in advanced cases
- Reduced oil content in affected fruits
- Entire bunch may be lost

### Cause & Transmission
- High humidity + warm temperatures (>28°C)
- Insect damage creating entry points
- Poor sanitation in field
- Waterlogged conditions
- Fruit damage during harvest
- Spores present on dead organic matter

### Treatment
- **Field Sanitation**: Remove rotted bunches immediately
- **Dispose**: Burn or deep bury affected bunches (NOT compost heap)
- **Fungicide (Preventive)**: 
  * Azoxystrobin (0.04%) - systemic, effective
  * Trichoderma spray before harvest
- **Early Harvest**: Harvest slightly early if rot detected
- **Harvest During Dry**: Afternoon harvesting reduces moisture

### Prevention
1. Improve drainage (critical for bunch rot)
2. Avoid overhead irrigation near bunches
3. Pest management (insects create entry wounds)
4. Harvest with clean tools
5. Sanitize pruning tools between trees
6. Remove all dead plant matter monthly
7. Space palms for air circulation
8. Apply preventive fungicides in high-risk season

### Yield Impact
- Untreated: 20-30% loss during high-humidity season
- With prevention: <5% loss
- Quality: Affected fruit produces low-quality oil

## 4. Pest Infestations - Major Pests of Oil Palm

### A. Rhinoceros Beetle (*Oryctes rhinoceros*)
- **Damage**: Larvae tunnel through trunk (frass visible)
- **Impact**: Severe wilting, crown death, total yield loss
- **Control**: Pheromone traps, parasitic nematodes (Steinernema), proper sanitation
- **Loss**: 100% if large larvae present in crown

### B. Bagworm (*Metisa plana*)
- **Damage**: Severe defoliation, skeletal leaves remain
- **Impact**: 30-50% yield loss if untreated
- **Control**: Pruning infected fronds, Bacillus thuringiensis (BT), natural predators
- **Prevention**: Maintain good canopy health

### C. Scale Insects
- **Damage**: Feeding on fronds, wilting
- **Impact**: 10-20% yield loss
- **Control**: Insecticidal soap, neem oil
- **Threshold**: >5 per frond requires action

### D. Mites (Spider mites, *Oligonychus*) 
- **Damage**: Fine webbing, yellowing fronds
- **Impact**: Reduced photosynthesis
- **Control**: Water spray (disrupts webbing), miticides if severe
- **Threshold**: >20 mites per leaflet

### E. Stem Borers
- **Damage**: Tunnel through trunk, creating holes
- **Impact**: Structural weakness, sudden collapse
- **Control**: Sanitize tools, remove infected wood, burn
- **Threshold**: >3 holes visible

### Pest Management Strategy
1. **Regular scouting** (monthly during season)
2. **Economic threshold** concept (treat when population reaches threshold)
3. **Integrated Pest Management** (IPM):
   - Cultural control (sanitation, proper spacing)
   - Mechanical control (manual removal, traps)
   - Biological control (predators, parasitoids, entomopathogenic fungi)
   - Chemical control (last resort, selective use)
4. **Pheromone monitoring** for early detection
5. **Population tracking** to predict outbreaks

## 5. Nutritional Deficiencies (Abiotic Stresses)

### Nitrogen (N) Deficiency
- **Symptoms**: Uniform yellowing of fronds, reduced bunch size
- **Treatment**: Urea (0.5-1.0 kg/palm/year) or NP fertilizer
- **Yield Impact**: 20-30% reduction

### Potassium (K) Deficiency
- **Symptoms**: Orangish frond tips, reduced oil content
- **Treatment**: KCl (1-2 kg/palm/year)
- **Yield Impact**: 15-25% reduction

### Magnesium (Mg) Deficiency
- **Symptoms**: Interveinal chlorosis on lower fronds (orange-red)
- **Treatment**: Epsom salt (0.3 kg/palm/year) or foliar Mg spray
- **Yield Impact**: 10-15% reduction

### Boron (B) Deficiency
- **Symptoms**: Translucent spots, frond distortion
- **Treatment**: Borax (0.05-0.1 kg/palm/year)
- **Yield Impact**: Structural damage, poor pollination

### Nutrient Management Best Practices
1. Soil testing every 2-3 years
2. Tissue analysis for diagnosis (critical fronds)
3. Balanced NPK ratios (1:0.5:1.2 typical)
4. Organic matter incorporation (mulching)
5. Fractional application of fertilizers
6. Micronutrient supplementation in deficient soils

## 6. Environmental Stresses

### Drought Stress
- Oil palm requires 2000-2500mm/year
- Irrigate if <1500mm/year
- Mulching essential in dry seasons
- Yield loss: 20-40% under severe drought

### Waterlogging
- Primary risk factor for Ganoderma
- Install proper drainage systems
- Avoid in basins or low-lying areas
- Can cause 50% yield loss

### Temperature Extremes
- Optimal: 24-32°C
- Below 20°C: Pollination fails
- Above 35°C: Stress, frond burning
- Cold impacts: 10-20% yield reduction

## 7. Certified Best Management Practices

### RSPO (Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil) Standards
- Mandatory environmental impact assessment
- Water management and pollution control
- Pesticide management (no banned pesticides)
- Soil conservation and rehabilitation
- Biodiversity conservation (10% of plantation as buffer/wildlife)
- Worker health and safety standards

### ISCC (International Sustainability & Carbon Certification)
- Greenhouse gas emission calculations
- Renewable energy use requirements
- Traceability throughout supply chain
- Contamination prevention standards

### Disease Monitoring Protocol (CRITICAL)
1. **Monthly inspections** of 25-30 palms per 100 hectares
2. **Record-keeping**: Disease type, location, severity (0-5 scale)
3. **Action thresholds**:
   - GBSR: 1% infected → take action
   - Leaf spot: >30% frond affected → spray
   - Bunch rot: >5% bunches affected → harvest early
4. **Quarterly reporting** to management
5. **Historical trending** for predictive management
